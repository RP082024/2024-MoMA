MoMA - The Quest for Diversity

A study on a museums acquisition policy.



In 2019 the Museum of Modern Art (MoMA) closed its doors for four months to address its lack of diversity and representation of artists of different genders and nationalities within their works on view.

After the reopening, Jason Farago describes in the New York Times how putting Picasso and Monet next to more recent, diverse artists changed the MoMA experience.

Images: (left) Henri Matisse, 1909, Dance (I), MoMA; (right) Liubov Popova, 1917, Painterly Architectonic, MoMA



But did the acquisition practice change as well?

This study wants to determine whether the acquisition policy of the MoMA changed during the last five years.
It will also compare the diversity of the current exhibition with the entire museum collection to see if the selections show measurable differences.

For this purpose diversity measures, as they are widely used in ecology, are transferred to the cultural domain;
- to explore their applicability and usability,
- to gain new insights,
- and to enjoy the pleasures, challengies, uncertainties, and surprises of inter- and transdisciplinary endevors.


In the late 1920s, three progressive and influential patrons of the arts, Lillie P. Bliss, Mary Quinn Sullivan, and Abby Aldrich Rockefeller, perceived a need to challenge the conservative policies of traditional museums and to establish an institution devoted exclusively to modern art.

Citation: MoMA Through Time


Barr proposed selling works more than fifty years old to other museums as MoMA’s collection aged, while continuing to purchase work by living artists.

This radical approach was never implemented and is softened by the declaration, that modernity is still an ongoing process.
Citation: MoMA Through Time


Founding director Alfred H. Barr Jr. initially conceived the Museum’s collection as “a torpedo moving through time, its nose the ever advancing present, its tail the ever preceding past of 50 to 100 years ago.”

Citation: MoMA Through Time


The MoMA has faced frequent accusations of being exclusive, ruled by the Western culture, predominantly white and male, elitist, and paternalistic.

Citation: MoMA Through Time

For example: "In 1997 the Guerrilla Girls, a feminist artist collective, surveyed MoMA’s painting and sculpture galleries, and determined that a paltry 9 percent of the offerings were by white women; the survey found that not a single work on view was by a woman of color. Later on, the picture looked even worse. When art historian Maura Reilly surveyed the permanent galleries after a 2004 reinstallation, she found that just 4 percent of the art displayed was by women, and even less than that by women of color."

Big Changes Confirmed!

Diversity analysis indicates:

The current permanent exhibition is markedly more diverse than the complete collection, this must have been achieved by a thoughtful selection of paintings.

From 2019 to 2024, acquisition practices have undergone substantial changes, and the diversity index has increased significantly.











The collection at the MoMA is as varied and plentiful as expected, similar to how a zoo has more diverse and numerous species than typically occur within natural habitats.

World-renowned museums like the Louvre or the British Museum, known for their broad collections, have an estimated Standardized Shannon Index of approximately 80%-95% (CAVE: This might be a Chat-GPT halluzination and depends anyhow on the definition of species; - at least in the cultural domain).


The collection on view (aka permanent exhibition) shows a significantly higher degree of diversity than the complete collection. Far-reaching changes must have been made and thoughtful selection processes must have been applied to achieve this.


The acquisitions 2019-2024 show a remarkable increase in diversity, achieved by e.g. enhancing the early modernity collection with works by female artists and incorporating art from previously unrepresented or underrepresented cultural regions (e.g. cultural regions in Africa and Asia). However, the availability of high-quality works on the art market restricts these efforts, making the outcomes notable, albeit still short of desired goals.

What is achieved?
What shall be achieved?


In any case, MoMA's pursuit of diversity has broadened its appreciation for artworks outside its traditional scope, benefitting the museum, artists, researchers, and the public.


Let us confidently assume (and monitor!) that MoMA will steadfastly continue on this path.



For MoMA's collection to equally represent women and men, it would require solely acquiring women's artworks from the past 100 years for the next 75 years (extrapolating from the past acquisitions).
This strategy presupposes the availability of such artworks at costs as were typical at the time of their creation, and it means to stop collecting all contemporary artworks during this period.

And this would solely affect gender equality, neither e.g. cultural regions nor the time periods the artworks were created in!



But ...

A higher diversity in the paintings acquired within a short timeframe does not increase the diversity of the complete collection at one blow.

A collection is deliberately built, curated and presented with specific intentions.
Achieving a collection of paintings that equally represents all cultural regions and time periods is neither feasible nor advisable, if relevance and art historical significance are to be maintained.

Sure enough, this is not meant to be an excuse for ignoring or disregarding the accomplishments of women, of artists from non-Western cultures, etc.

Diversity

From Everyday Notion to Rigorous Statistical Measure

In conversational contexts, we often colloquially refer to diversity when we want to express that the community in question is not uniform.
However, uniformity can apply to a variety of aspects.

For instance, we might describe a workplace as diverse if its members come from several different countries, even though they may all be young. Similarly, we might call a club diverse if its members span a wide range of professions, overlooking the fact that all are white men.

Diversity depends on the viewpoint and on specific characteristics that are relevant to a certain community at a particular time and place.





According to Ann Magurran (1988) the Shannon Index of Diversity is one of the most widely used information theory indices.
In ecology most often the diversity of species is measured, while both the number of species (also known as species richness) and the relative abundance (the proportion of individuals belonging to each species) are considered equally.

Higher values indicate higher diversity. A diversity of 0 indicates the presence of one cultural species solely.

S is the set of all cultural species.
The summation symbol indicates, that we need to sum up all values of the expression.
pi is the proportion of each cultural species relative to the total number of paintings.
ln(pi) the natural logarithm function scales the proportions in a way that diminishes the effect of extremly common or extremly rare cultural species.

Step 1: Collect the Data

Suppose an art historian wants to determine the diversity of paintings at a museum.
She creates 'cultural species' and counts the paintings, that fall into each category.


She has collected 105 paintings belonging to 5 'cultural species'

Step 2: Calculate the Proportions

The data set holds 105 paintings. 40 belong to 'cultural species' A. The art historian calculates 40/105 to get the proportion. 0.38 (or 38%) of the whole collection belong to 'cultural species' A.


105 paintings belonging to 5 'cultural species' with shares between 8 and 38%.

Step 3: Calculate the Natural Log of the Proportions

Now she can calculate the natural log of the proportions. This helps to diminish the effect of extremly common or extremly rare 'cultural species'.


This step produces negative values, that's why the Shannon formula has a minus sign at the beginning.

Step 4: Multiply the Proportions by the Natural Log of the Proportions

Looking at the formula: we need to multiplicate column 3 and 4.


Our hypothetical art historian is almost done with the calculations!

Step 5: Calculate the Shannon Diversity Index

Now she takes the sum of the last column and multiplies it by negative one.


Finally! The Shannon Diversity Index (H) was calculated to be 1.49.

Step 6: Standardization
for Comparability Across Collections

The Shannon Equitability Index is calculated by dividing the Shannon Index (H) by the natural logarithm of the number of 'cultural species' present, ln⁡(#’cultural species’), aka by the maximum possible value. This standardization allows for comparability of the index across different collections.
The standardized Shannon Diversity Index (H), also known as the Shannon Equitability Index was calculated to be 92% (EH = 1.49 / ln(5) = 0.92).

There are a lot of diversity measures and standardization methods out there.
This is just one example, that is not even best suited for the purpose of comparing collections' diversity (brillouin index might be better suited to that task), but was chosen as an easy way to showcase the approach.

To learn more about diversity, please refer to the references at the end of the website.



The Formation of "Cultural Species"


This study adapts the concept of diversity measurement to the cultural domain by establishing "Cultural Species" for the paintings at the Museum of Modern Art (MoMA).

In Art History, the period and cultural context of an artwork's creation are crucial for assessing its significance and its role in the evolution of art. Consequently, these two pieces of metadata were included.

Additionally, the gender and age of the artists at the time of creating the respective artworks were included to capture more personal characteristics of the artists involved.
